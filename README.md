Where the 13 Hours Went
Troubleshooting a Class D amplifier is far more difficult than a standard Class AB "linear" amp. Because it relies on high-frequency switching, you can't just check for "clean" voltage; you are chasing ghosts in the nanosecond range.

The "Hiccup" Mode: The IRS2092 has built-in Over-Current Protection (OCP). If it detects even a tiny mismatch in the MOSFET timing or a slight solder bridge, it enters a "hiccup" cycle where it tries to start and immediately shuts down.
Heat vs. Logic: Repeatedly swapping components like the bootstrap capacitor and gate resistors consumed hours. Every time a component is desoldered and resoldered, you risk damaging the delicate PCB pads.
<img width="1915" height="1080" alt="Screenshot_20260513-214530" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/5d7244b8-62bb-4485-b8b5-79d67f1f9296" />

Signal Integrity: Using an oscilloscope to check the PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) signal is essential. However, the noise generated by the switching can often make it difficult to see if the chip is actually failing or if your probes are picking up interference.
Why It Didn’t Work (The Post-Mortem)
After 13 hours of rework, the board remained non-functional. In electronics, "Failure" is just a different kind of data. Potential culprits for this specific build failure include:
Internal IC Damage: It is possible the IRS2092S was damaged during the initial power-on or through electrostatic discharge (ESD).

Hidden Shorts: With SMT components, a microscopic bridge under the IC or a MOSFET can be impossible to see without an X-ray or removing the chip entirely.
Trace Impedance: At 500W, even the resistance of a PCB trace can cause a voltage drop that triggers the protection logic.

Lessons for Your Next Build
If you are planning to tackle a high-power Class D project, 
keep these tips in mind to avoid the 13-hour trap:
Use a Current-Limiter: Always use a "dim bulb" tester or a current-limited lab power supply for the first power-up.

Check the Bootstrap: The high-side driver is the most common point of failure. If your bootstrap cap isn't perfect, the amp will never start.

Know When to Walk Away: Sometimes, the best troubleshooting tool is a full night of sleep.
Failure is part of the engineering process. Every hour spent at the bench—even if the LED doesn't turn green—is an hour spent getting better at the craft.
